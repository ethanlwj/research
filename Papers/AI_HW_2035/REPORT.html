Easy-to-Read Report: AI+HW 2035: Shaping the Next Decade

Title: AI+HW 2035: Shaping the Next Decade

Authors: Deming Chen et al.

arXiv: 2603.05225 (v1, March 5, 2026)

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05225

1) One-Paragraph Summary

This paper argues that AI progress over the next 10 years depends on tight co-design of AI algorithms and hardware. Current AI growth is increasingly limited by energy use, memory bandwidth, interconnect bottlenecks, and deployment cost, not just raw compute. The authors propose a roadmap toward a 1000x efficiency improvement (measured as intelligence per joule) by combining model innovation, memory-centric hardware, better system software, and coordinated academia-industry-government collaboration.

2) Why This Paper Matters

  • Brute-force scaling is becoming too expensive and power-hungry.
  • Key bottlenecks are shifting from arithmetic to data movement, memory, and networking.
  • AI and hardware are often developed in silos, creating a speed mismatch.
  • Future success depends on end-to-end efficiency, deployability, and trustworthiness.

3) Main Thesis

Progress should be measured by useful intelligence per unit energy/cost/time. That requires co-evolving models, compilers, runtimes, systems, and hardware together, rather than optimizing each layer independently.

  • Compute-centric design -> memory-centric and system-centric design
  • Single-layer optimization -> cross-layer co-design
  • Peak benchmark focus -> deployment metrics (latency, energy, utilization, robustness, cost)

4) Core Ideas by Section

Hardware technologies: Prioritize memory/data-movement efficiency, interconnect scaling, 3D integration, adaptive hardware, and AI-driven design automation.

Algorithms/paradigms: Smaller domain-tuned models, better memory-locality-aware designs, distinct training vs inference targets, and self-optimizing software stacks.

Applications/society: Major impact in robotics, healthcare, science, climate, and infrastructure; risks include power constraints, fragmentation, and unequal access.

5) The 1000x Efficiency Claim

The paper targets roughly 100x in 5 years (high confidence) and 1000x in 6-10 years (moderate confidence), based on combined gains from algorithms, silicon/utilization, and system-level optimization.

  • ~10x from model/algorithm improvements
  • ~20x from silicon utilization and hardware advances
  • ~5x from system-level efficiency

6) What Success Looks Like by 2035

  • Interoperable heterogeneous systems across accelerators and platforms
  • Much lower energy per useful output and higher sustained utilization
  • Reliable support for physical AI (real-time and power-constrained)
  • Small and large models working together in production
  • Faster hardware design cycles via AI-assisted EDA
  • Broader access through shared infrastructure and standards

7) Risks and Open Challenges

  • Thermal and reliability limits in dense 3D integration
  • Noise/calibration issues in analog and photonic paths
  • Software portability across heterogeneous hardware
  • Over-specialization and reduced flexibility
  • Fleet-level reliability and silent data corruption
  • Energy infrastructure and policy lag

8) Bottom Line

This is a strategy/vision paper rather than an experimental benchmark paper. Its main contribution is a coordinated long-term roadmap for AI and hardware together. The key message: the next decade of AI progress depends on jointly optimizing capability, energy, cost, reliability, and societal impact, instead of scaling compute in isolation.
