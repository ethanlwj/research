2. Context and Problem Statement

The paper is written as a 10-year roadmap responding to a structural mismatch: model growth and software iteration are accelerating faster than hardware and infrastructure cycles, while energy, memory movement, and interconnect constraints are becoming hard limits. Rather than treating hardware as a downstream implementation detail, the paper argues that future AI capability depends on integrated co-evolution across the full stack.

"Looking ahead, we define what success means in 10 years: achieving a 1000x improvement in efficiency for AI training and inference..." (p.2)

From this premise, the central research problem can be stated as follows: how should the AI ecosystem redesign models, hardware, systems software, and deployment infrastructure to achieve major capability gains under strict energy, latency, reliability, and scalability constraints?

3. Main Points and Insights (With Exposition)

  • Data movement is the limiting resource in modern AI stacks.

    The paper emphasizes that energy spent moving data now often dominates energy spent computing. This reframes optimization priorities from arithmetic throughput to data locality and memory hierarchy behavior. The implication is significant: algorithm design, architecture design, and runtime scheduling must all expose and optimize memory traffic explicitly, otherwise model scaling will continue to produce diminishing practical returns.

  • Memory-centric and 3D-integrated hardware is presented as a strategic path, not a niche option.

    Compute-in/near-memory, dense 3D integration, and advanced packaging are framed as key enablers of lower-latency, lower-energy AI execution. The argument is that closer physical coupling of logic-memory-interconnect can unlock dataflows that planar, compute-centric designs cannot efficiently support. The paper also notes trade-offs, especially thermal constraints, manufacturability, and system complexity.

  • Interconnect design becomes co-equal with core compute design.

    The paper treats network topology, bandwidth density, startup latency, and protocol overhead as central to scalable training and inference. In this view, AI scaling bottlenecks move from "compute scarcity" to "communication inefficiency" at cluster and fleet scales, motivating optical interconnect exploration and topology-aware co-design between workloads and infrastructure.

  • The report separates training and inference as distinct engineering regimes.

    Training is characterized as throughput/efficiency intensive, while inference - especially in embodied or safety-constrained deployments - is latency-, power-, and determinism-constrained. This distinction matters because one hardware/software stack or benchmark philosophy is unlikely to optimize both simultaneously. The paper uses this split to justify multi-metric evaluation rather than single-score benchmarking.

  • Algorithmic innovation is treated as a multiplicative force on hardware progress.

    The authors do not claim hardware advances alone will deliver long-horizon targets. Instead, they propose compounding gains from model design (distillation, sparsity, architecture alternatives, memory-aware execution), silicon utilization improvements, and fleet/system optimization. This framing is important because it makes end-to-end efficiency a systems property emerging from multiple layers.

  • Low real-world utilization is identified as a major practical gap.

    The paper reports that deployments often run far below peak hardware capacity, pointing to compiler/runtime immaturity, heterogeneity friction, and scheduling inefficiencies. The proposed response is AI-assisted automation for kernel synthesis, optimization loops, and fleet telemetry-driven control, effectively extending co-design into operations.

    "In real deployments, systems often operate at only 5-20% utilization." (Sec. 4.3, p.19)

  • Power infrastructure is positioned as a first-order AI constraint.

    Beyond chips and models, the paper makes an infrastructure-level claim: datacenter and grid expansion may not keep pace with AI demand. This broadens AI+HW research from technical co-design to energy planning and policy alignment, and it links national competitiveness to available power capacity in addition to algorithmic quality.

  • Institutional coordination is cast as a technical enabler.

    The paper's recommendations for academia-industry-government collaboration are not only governance statements; they are intended to unlock shared benchmarks, legal distillation pathways, open tooling, and reproducible evaluation. The underlying insight is that fragmented incentives can block technical progress even when component technologies exist.

5. Key Contributions

  • Integrated AI+HW roadmap across technical and institutional layers.

    The paper's strongest contribution is synthesis: it connects algorithms, architecture, runtimes, infrastructure, and policy into one framework, which is often missing in more narrowly scoped technical papers.

  • Explicit milestone framing with system-level metrics.

    By introducing utilization, intelligence-per-joule, and energy efficiency milestones, the paper advances evaluation criteria beyond model accuracy and peak throughput, encouraging deployment-relevant accountability.

  • Challenge taxonomy and opportunity mapping.

    The paper provides a structured mapping from bottlenecks (memory wall, interconnect limits, thermal constraints, design complexity) to candidate interventions (memory-centric compute, optical links, AI-driven EDA, fleet optimization), which can guide research agenda setting.

  • Pragmatic recognition of operations and infrastructure constraints.

    The inclusion of low utilization, power constraints, and cloud-edge workload shifts makes the roadmap operationally grounded relative to purely architectural visions.

6. Gaps and Limitations

  • Empirical validation is less developed than strategic framing.

    Many key numerical claims are plausible but presented as directional forecasts. The paper would be stronger with controlled cross-layer studies quantifying marginal gains and interactions under standardized workloads.

  • Implementation sequencing is under-specified.

    The roadmap spans many interventions but does not fully specify dependencies, order of operations, or contingency plans when specific technologies (for example, optical integration or standardized telemetry) mature slower than expected.

  • Reproducibility mechanisms remain aspirational.

    The paper calls for multi-metric reporting and openness, but practical mechanisms for shared telemetry, legal interoperability, and cross-institution benchmark governance are not fully operationalized in the manuscript.

  • Policy and geopolitics claims may require periodic recalibration.

    Infrastructure and national competitiveness arguments are important but sensitive to rapid external changes. Their policy relevance depends on frequently updated energy and capacity datasets.

7. Overall Assessment

AI+HW 2035 is best read as a strategic research roadmap rather than an experimental proof paper. Its value lies in articulating a coherent co-design agenda with concrete milestones and a broad systems perspective. Its main weakness is that key forecasts are not yet tied to a fully reproducible empirical protocol. Even so, as a planning document for AI-hardware research programs, it provides high utility by clarifying where cross-layer integration is likely to produce the largest next-decade gains.

"We predict 100x gains in 5 years (high confidence) and 1000x in 6-10 years (moderate confidence)..." (Sec. 5.2, p.25)
