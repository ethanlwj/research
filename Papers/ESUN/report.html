Technical Report

ESUN's latest changes and insights

Prepared on 2026-03-16. Primary source: OCP ESUN - Network Operator Requirements - Base Specification Rev 1.0, effective February 9, 2026 and released by OCP on March 10, 2026.

Executive summary

ESUN, short for Ethernet for Scale-Up Networks, is an Open Compute Project workstream that adapts Ethernet for tightly coupled AI accelerator domains. The Rev 1.0 base specification makes a clear architectural move: shrink protocol overhead, keep Ethernet's ecosystem advantages, and reintroduce the lost control signals in a compact ESUN header. The result is a scale-up fabric profile optimized for low latency, lossless delivery, traffic differentiation, and resilience across hierarchical multi-hop topologies.

The most important technical changes are the new 4-byte ESUN header, explicit support for EH-ECN, EH-CoS, EH-Flow Label, and EH-TTL, plus required switch support for PFC, CBFC, and LLR. Taken together, these changes reposition Ethernet from a general-purpose data center interconnect into a more specialized operator-friendly fabric for large GPU memory domains.

Report scope

This report focuses on two threads:

  • Background and context: ESUN's inception, history, purpose, and operator/business goals.
  • Technical synthesis: insights and conclusions from the Rev 1.0 specification on QoS, congestion control, and low latency.

Notes on business goals are partly inferred from OCP's stated focus areas, tenets, and requirement language, and are marked as such.

1. Background and context

1.1 What ESUN is

ESUN is an OCP networking workstream and specification family focused on the network side of scale-up AI interconnects: protocol headers, switching behavior, lossless transport behavior, error handling, and multi-hop operation. OCP distinguishes this from endpoint-side transport concerns, which are handled by the separate SUE-Transport effort. In practical terms, ESUN aims to make Ethernet viable for GPU scale-up domains that previously depended on more tightly specialized interconnects or custom designs.

1.2 Inception and short history

Date Milestone Why it matters
April 29, 2025 OCP and the UALink Consortium announced collaboration on scale-up interconnect performance for AI and HPC clusters. This set the broader market context: open, high-bandwidth, low-latency scale-up fabrics had become a strategic industry priority.
October 13, 2025 OCP publicly announced the ESUN workstream at the OCP Global Summit 2025. ESUN formally began as an open forum for operators and vendors to adapt Ethernet for scale-up AI networking, with 12 founding members including Meta, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Oracle, AMD, Arista, Arm, Broadcom, Cisco, HPE Networking, and Marvell.
February 9, 2026 The Rev 1.0 base specification became effective. This is the first baseline operator requirements document and establishes the functional profile for compliant endpoints and switches.
March 10, 2026 OCP announced the release of the ESUN 1.0 specification. The workstream moved from kickoff to a published base specification in roughly four months, signaling urgency and strong ecosystem alignment.

1.3 Business and operator goals

Explicit goals from the sources

The specification repeatedly frames ESUN around hyperscale AI infrastructure needs: improved goodput, lower latency, higher resilience, differentiated traffic handling, and better GPU utilization. OCP's supporting materials also emphasize interoperability, ecosystem enablement, and standards alignment with IEEE and UEC.

Inferred business goals

The underlying business case is to reduce dependence on proprietary scale-up fabrics, shorten deployment cycles, preserve Ethernet supply-chain economics, and improve return on expensive accelerator clusters by cutting network-induced idle time.

Rev 1.0 presents ESUN as an evolution of Ethernet, not a clean-sheet replacement. That positioning matters commercially: it preserves familiarity for operators and silicon vendors, while still introducing enough structure to target scale-up GPU fabrics with more deterministic performance than conventional Ethernet deployments usually provide.

By March 10, 2026, OCP reported that ESUN had grown to more than 175 participating companies. That is a strong signal that the initiative is not merely a bilateral Meta-Microsoft document, but an ecosystem effort aimed at creating a broadly deployable market option.

1.4 Why ESUN appeared now

The specification says scale-up traffic is dominated by model-parallel communication, which demands both high bandwidth and low latency. It also notes a transition from 8-GPU single-node systems toward next-generation domains of 1,000 or more GPUs spanning racks. In that setting, even modest bandwidth degradation translates into meaningful end-to-end training loss and higher annual operating cost. ESUN therefore responds to a concrete operational problem: standard Ethernet capabilities are helpful, but alone they do not cleanly fit the latency, goodput, and resilience envelope needed for large AI memory domains.

2. Latest changes introduced by ESUN Rev 1.0

Area Standard Ethernet baseline ESUN Rev 1.0 change Operational impact
Header model IPv4/IPv6 headers carry ECN, DSCP, TTL, and hashing entropy. Removes 20-40 bytes of IP header and adds a compact 4-byte ESUN header. Higher goodput for small messages and less protocol overhead on latency-sensitive traffic.
QoS DSCP and 802.1p normally classify traffic. EH-CoS adds 3-bit class signaling inside the ESUN header and takes precedence when present. Traffic class survives header reduction without relying on VLAN tagging.
Persistent congestion feedback IP-based ECN EH-ECN recreates ECN semantics inside the ESUN header. Congestion feedback remains available after IP removal.
Transient congestion control PFC PFC remains required and ESUN adds required switch support for CBFC. Lossless behavior is preserved while enabling a more memory-efficient switch design path.
Resilience No Ethernet-native link-level retry beyond FEC LLR is introduced for switch resiliency and optional host support. Shortens exposure to BER-induced packet loss on high-speed links.
Load balancing Entropy often comes from IP headers and sockets. EH-Flow Label plus the F validity bit provide deterministic hashing input. Helps retain balanced multi-path forwarding after IP header removal.
Loop control IP-TTL EH-TTL is decremented on each hop and packets are dropped at zero. Maintains multi-hop safety in an L2-forwarded ESUN domain.

3. Technical synthesis: QoS, congestion control, and low latency

3.1 QoS and traffic differentiation

ESUN does not use the phrase "QoS" as a formal section title, but it clearly implements a QoS model through traffic differentiation, class mapping, and congestion signaling. The central mechanism is EH-CoS, a 3-bit class-of-service field intended to recover the traffic-class semantics that would otherwise be lost when the IP header is removed. The specification explicitly says EH-CoS should take precedence over class markings from 802.1Q or CBFC when all are present.

This reveals an important design choice: ESUN wants the packet itself to carry its scale-up intent in a compact and unambiguous way. Rather than treating QoS as an external VLAN or IP policy overlay, Rev 1.0 moves traffic class closer to the forwarding plane. That makes sense for tightly controlled GPU fabrics, where the operator values deterministic and low-overhead classification more than broad Internet-style flexibility.

Insight

ESUN's QoS model is intentionally narrow and fabric-centric. It is not trying to become a universal policy framework. It is trying to give scale-up AI networks just enough differentiation to separate critical traffic classes without reintroducing the full overhead of the IP stack.

3.2 Congestion control architecture

Rev 1.0 treats congestion as a two-timescale problem. For transient link-level congestion, it retains PFC and adds CBFC as an additional mechanism recommended for memory-efficient switch implementations. For persistent fabric congestion, it restores ECN semantics through EH-ECN. This is a notable architectural split: short-term pause/credit controls manage losslessness locally, while explicit marking feeds longer control loops back to endpoints.

The product requirements reinforce this split. Endpoints must support PFC and ESUN header processing, including responding to ECN and advertising capability. Switches must support PFC, CBFC, and LLR, be ECN-capable, use flow labels for hashing when valid, and decrement TTL. In other words, ESUN assigns most of the fabric enforcement burden to the switch while still requiring endpoint participation in closed-loop congestion response.

What is new

The main innovation is not inventing brand-new congestion theory. It is preserving the useful Ethernet mechanisms while relocating them into an ESUN-specific packet and switch profile that remains efficient after IP removal.

Why it matters

Large GPU fabrics suffer when congestion either drops packets or creates long stalls. ESUN's layered approach tries to reduce both risks: lossless behavior for immediate pressure, ECN-style signals for sustained overload, and load balancing to avoid hot spots.

Analytical note: Rev 1.0 assumes that lossless mechanisms such as PFC and CBFC can be deployed in a disciplined way. The document does not deeply analyze tradeoffs like pause propagation or head-of-line blocking. That means successful operation will depend on implementation quality, buffer design, and careful class isolation, even though those details are outside the base specification.

3.3 Low-latency strategy

Low latency is one of the strongest themes in the document. The specification ties latency directly to model developer productivity, GPU stall avoidance, distributed training speed, and the trend toward smaller message sizes across larger domains. ESUN's latency strategy has three layers.

  • Protocol efficiency: removing 20-40 bytes of IP header and replacing it with a 4-byte ESUN header improves goodput and lowers per-packet overhead, especially for small messages.
  • Congestion avoidance and recovery: low latency is protected indirectly through lossless controls, ECN feedback, LLR, and load balancing so packets do not queue or drop excessively in hierarchical fabrics.
  • Forwarding determinism: static destination-address forwarding, flow-label hashing, and TTL-based loop control reduce the chance that multi-hop L2 behavior becomes erratic as the domain scales.

The deeper insight is that ESUN sees latency as a systems property, not only a serialization-delay problem. Header shrinkage helps, but the specification puts equal emphasis on avoiding stalls, hot spots, retransmission-driven delay, and unstable scaling behavior. That is the right framing for AI scale-up networks, where a slightly slower packet stream can idle a much more expensive compute pipeline.

3.4 What the spec implies for implementation

Implementation implication Reasoning from the spec
Switch ASICs become more central to ESUN success. Switches carry mandatory support for PFC, CBFC, LLR, ECN marking, TTL updates, DA-based forwarding, and flow-label-aware hashing.
Control-plane discipline is required. ESUN assumes static destination-address programming and explicitly says unknown-address ESUN packets should not be broadcast.
Packet mutation in flight has hardware cost. The spec notes that updating ECN or TTL requires FCS recomputation during forwarding.
ESUN is strongest in managed domains, not open enterprise networks. The design choices favor deterministic, operator-controlled AI fabrics over general-purpose interoperability across loosely managed networks.

4. Conclusions

ESUN Rev 1.0 is best understood as a focused Ethernet profile for AI scale-up fabrics. Its latest changes do not try to replace Ethernet's ecosystem; instead they compress and specialize it. The compact ESUN header restores the minimum control fields needed for class-based treatment, congestion feedback, load balancing, and loop control after removing IP overhead.

From a technical standpoint, the most important conclusion is that QoS, congestion control, and low latency are tightly coupled in ESUN. QoS without ECN and pause behavior would not keep the fabric lossless. Congestion control without compact differentiation would undermine efficiency. Low latency without load balancing and resilience would collapse at larger scale. Rev 1.0 therefore packages these as a single operator profile rather than isolated features.

From an industry standpoint, ESUN signals that open Ethernet is being pushed upward into territory traditionally associated with specialized accelerator interconnects. If implementations deliver on the specification's intent, ESUN could lower the cost and complexity of building large GPU memory domains while preserving openness, interoperability, and vendor diversity.
