Technical Report

Broadcom and ESUN: Latest Changes and Implications

Prepared on March 16, 2026. This report centers on the latest public ESUN baseline, OCP ESUN - Network Operator Requirements - Base Specification Rev 1.0, effective February 9, 2026 and announced by OCP on March 10, 2026. In this report, “Broadcom ESUN” is interpreted as Broadcom's role and implications within the OCP ESUN effort, because the specification itself is an OCP multi-company document rather than a Broadcom-only specification.

Executive Summary

ESUN, short for Ethernet for Scale-Up Networks, is OCP's attempt to evolve Ethernet into a credible fabric for tightly coupled AI scale-up domains. The latest change is not a minor tuning item. Rev 1.0 defines a baseline architectural profile: remove the 20-40 byte IP header, add a compact 4-byte ESUN Header, preserve Ethernet deployment simplicity, and reintroduce the critical control signals needed for scale-up fabrics such as ECN, CoS, Flow Label, and TTL.

The most important technical message is that ESUN does not chase low latency with one mechanism. It combines header efficiency, explicit traffic differentiation, transient congestion control, persistent congestion signaling, and multi-hop resilience into one operator-oriented fabric model.

Why Broadcom matters here

Broadcom appears in two important places. First, it was listed among the initial companies when OCP launched the ESUN workstream on October 13, 2025. Second, Broadcom contributors are acknowledged in the Rev 1.0 specification itself. That makes Broadcom an ecosystem shaper, not just a passive observer.

1. Origin, history, background, and business purpose

1.1 What ESUN is trying to solve

According to the Rev 1.0 base specification, ESUN exists because scale-up GPU networks increasingly need to support model-parallel traffic with very high bandwidth and very low latency across larger domains. The document explicitly states that applications are trending toward smaller message sizes, larger GPU domains, and stricter latency sensitivity. This is the immediate technical background.

From an operator perspective, ESUN is about making Ethernet viable inside AI scale-up fabrics where GPU stalls, queue build-up, and instability quickly convert into training slowdown and higher infrastructure cost. The specification even gives a business-style example: a 20% bandwidth drop in a 128-accelerator domain can cause about 5% end-to-end performance loss and a higher annual operating cost.

1.2 Short timeline

Date Milestone Meaning
April 29, 2025 OCP and the UALink Consortium announced collaboration around scale-up interconnects for AI and HPC. This set the industry backdrop: open high-bandwidth, low-latency scale-up fabrics had become a strategic priority.
October 13, 2025 OCP introduced the ESUN workstream at OCP Global Summit 2025. ESUN formally became an open technical forum for adapting Ethernet to scale-up AI networking.
February 9, 2026 Rev 1.0 base specification became effective. The first operator requirements baseline was frozen.
March 10, 2026 OCP announced the release of the ESUN 1.0 specification. The workstream moved from concept to published baseline in roughly four months.

1.3 Broadcom's place in the story

Broadcom was one of the initial companies named in the OCP ESUN launch announcement, alongside hyperscalers and major switch or silicon vendors. In the Rev 1.0 acknowledgment section, Broadcom representatives are explicitly listed. This suggests Broadcom is participating where the core merchant-ethernet value chain meets the new AI scale-up fabric requirements.

Inference From a business standpoint, ESUN is attractive to Broadcom because it protects and upgrades the Ethernet switching model for AI scale-up deployments, instead of letting that segment drift entirely toward proprietary or vertically integrated fabrics. In other words, ESUN helps keep merchant Ethernet relevant in the most performance-sensitive part of AI infrastructure.

1.4 Commercial purpose and operator motivation

What the sources say directly

  • Improve goodput, latency, and reliability for scale-up GPU connectivity.
  • Enable lossless, differentiated, and resilient hierarchical topologies.
  • Preserve openness, interoperability, and backward compatibility.
  • Reduce the need for custom hardware and custom firmware integration.

What this means commercially

  • Lower deployment friction for hyperscalers and large enterprises building AI clusters.
  • Create a common target for switch ASIC vendors, NIC vendors, and system vendors.
  • Shorten time to market for Ethernet-based AI fabrics.
  • Expand the addressable market for Ethernet silicon in scale-up, not only scale-out.

2. What is new in the latest ESUN baseline

2.1 Core architectural shift

The central change is the shift from standard IP-based Ethernet encapsulation to a compact L2-oriented ESUN forwarding model. Rev 1.0 removes the IP header, saves 20-40 bytes, and adds a 4-byte ESUN Header. The document presents this as a direct path to higher goodput efficiency across message sizes.

2.2 New control fields in the ESUN Header

Field Role Why it matters
EH-ECN Fabric congestion feedback Restores persistent congestion signaling after removing the IP header.
EH-CoS Traffic differentiation Provides a compact traffic-class signal for QoS-like behavior in the scale-up domain.
F + EH-Flow Label Load-balancing entropy Supports deterministic forwarding decisions and better balancing across paths.
EH-TTL Loop control across multiple hops Makes the L2-oriented design operable in hierarchical multi-hop topologies.

2.3 Required or emphasized fabric features

Rev 1.0 requires PFC, requires CBFC on switches and makes it optional on hosts, requires LLR on switches and makes it optional on hosts, and requires EH-ECN, EH-CoS, EH-Flow Label, and EH-TTL. Switches must also be ECN-capable, must decrement TTL during forwarding, and should use the flow label for hashing when the valid bit is set.

3. QoS insights

3.1 ESUN does not use the term QoS as a marketing banner, but it clearly defines a QoS control plane

The specification talks about traffic differentiation rather than a full traditional QoS framework, but the substance is there. EH-CoS is a 3-bit reduced form of the IP-layer DSCP intent, and the switch requirements state that when multiple CoS indications exist, EH-CoS should take precedence. That is a very important design choice.

3.2 Interpretation

  • ESUN is moving classification closer to the fabric-native header, instead of depending on IP constructs.
  • The design favors low-overhead, deterministic class signaling over richer but heavier policy expression.
  • This is appropriate for scale-up AI fabrics, where traffic patterns are narrower and more engineered than in general-purpose data centers.

3.3 Key conclusion on QoS

ESUN's QoS philosophy is not “more knobs.” It is “fewer, fabric-relevant knobs that survive a low-overhead L2 design.” For Broadcom and other switch vendors, this is good news because it aligns with hardware-efficient traffic-class handling rather than large software-defined policy stacks.

4. Congestion control insights

4.1 ESUN splits congestion into transient and persistent components

This is one of the strongest parts of the specification. Hierarchical topologies are described as requiring support for both transient congestion control and persistent congestion control. ESUN's answer is layered:

  • PFC and CBFC for link-level or transient congestion management.
  • EH-ECN for persistent congestion or fabric feedback.
  • EH-Flow Label plus load-balancing methods to help congestion avoidance, not just congestion reaction.

4.2 Why CBFC is notable

The specification says ESUN recommends Credit Based Flow Control, defined by UEC, because it can provide more memory-efficient switch designs. That matters commercially and architecturally. It means ESUN is not only trying to improve performance; it is trying to improve performance in a way that switch silicon can absorb economically.

4.3 Why this is better than a single-mechanism story

AI scale-up fabrics often suffer when a design relies on one magic control loop. ESUN is more realistic. It assumes that different congestion timescales require different mechanisms: fast local backpressure, fabric-visible marking, and smarter path selection. That is a healthier model for large multi-hop GPU domains.

4.4 Key conclusion on congestion control

ESUN's main congestion insight is architectural separation. It decouples immediate losslessness, persistent congestion visibility, and path-level balancing. This improves operability and makes the fabric easier to tune at scale. For Broadcom-class switching platforms, that separation fits well with hardware pipelines and queue-centric design.

5. Dynamic latency insights

5.1 The specification does not define a standalone “dynamic latency” algorithm

Rev 1.0 does not present a named feature called dynamic latency control. The better reading is that ESUN attacks latency under dynamic load through a combination of mechanisms: smaller headers, class-based differentiation, congestion signaling, load balancing, and multi-hop loop control.

5.2 Practical interpretation of dynamic latency in ESUN

Mechanism Latency effect Insight
4-byte ESUN Header Reduces protocol overhead and improves goodput Lower serialization and processing overhead matters more as message sizes shrink.
EH-CoS Helps keep latency-sensitive classes from being treated identically to bulk traffic Latency stability is partly a scheduling problem, not only a bandwidth problem.
EH-ECN Exposes persistent queue pressure before collapse Latency control improves when endpoints and transport can react before packet loss or widespread stalls.
EH-Flow Label Improves path selection and helps avoid hot spots Dynamic latency often comes from imbalance, not only from insufficient raw capacity.
EH-TTL and multi-hop support Protects correctness in hierarchical fabrics Stable latency requires bounded behavior under fault or misconfiguration, not just fast forwarding in the happy path.

5.3 Key conclusion on dynamic latency

ESUN is really aiming for latency predictability under changing load, not only low average latency. That is the right target for AI scale-up systems. GPU clusters care deeply about the tail: a stalled collective or straggling communication step can dominate job time. Rev 1.0 shows that ESUN understands this, even if it does not yet define a full latency-control framework.

6. Overall assessment and implications

6.1 What Rev 1.0 gets right

  • It is focused. The baseline is small enough to implement yet meaningful enough to shape the ecosystem.
  • It preserves Ethernet's openness while accepting that standard Ethernet alone is not enough for AI scale-up.
  • It treats low latency, congestion, load balancing, and resilience as one coupled problem.
  • It leaves room for future evolution through the revision field and explicit standards alignment with UEC and IEEE.

6.2 What remains open

  • The specification is a base requirement document, not the full operational playbook for large fabrics.
  • Host behavior is only partly defined, with some capabilities optional on endpoints.
  • Latency management policy, fairness policy, and end-to-end control-loop tuning still need ecosystem maturity.

6.3 Final takeaway

The latest ESUN change is best understood as a strategic repositioning of Ethernet for AI scale-up. For Broadcom, the implication is favorable: the market is asking for a more fabric-specialized Ethernet, not for Ethernet to disappear. For operators, the implication is equally clear: the path to scalable AI fabrics will depend less on generic Ethernet defaults and more on disciplined, hardware-supported control signals for class, congestion, resilience, and latency stability.
