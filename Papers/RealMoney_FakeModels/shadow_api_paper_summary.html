Real Money, Fake Models: Deceptive Model Claims in Shadow APIs

Easy-to-read summary report

ArXiv: 2603.01919 Version: v2 (Mar 5, 2026) Authors: Yage Zhang et al.

TL;DR

The paper audits unofficial “shadow APIs” that claim to serve frontier models (like GPT-5/Gemini-2.5) and finds frequent mismatch with official APIs. The authors report large utility drops, unstable safety behavior, and direct identity mismatch signals from fingerprinting. Their core conclusion: shadow APIs are not reliable substitutes for official APIs in research.

What the authors studied

  • Mapped the ecosystem: 17 shadow APIs found, linked to 187 research papers.
  • Benchmarked performance vs official APIs on science and sensitive tasks.
  • Audited safety behavior under jailbreak attacks.
  • Used model verification (LLM fingerprinting + MET statistical tests) to detect model substitution/deception.

Key findings (numbers)

47.21% Largest reported performance divergence (sensitive-domain setting).
45.83% Endpoints failed fingerprint identity verification.
+12.50% Additional endpoints with major cosine-distance deviations.
17 APIs / 187 papers Scope of shadow-API prevalence found by the study.
  • In medical/legal tasks, accuracy drops are severe for some models. Example: Gemini-2.5-flash on MedQA drops from 83.82% (official) to around 36.95% on tested shadow APIs.
  • Safety behavior is inconsistent: some shadow APIs understate or amplify harmfulness relative to official endpoints, which can invalidate safety evaluations.
  • Fingerprinting suggests common substitution patterns (e.g., premium model claims mapping to cheaper/open models in some cases).

Why this matters

  • Research reproducibility risk: results may not correspond to claimed models.
  • User risk: people can pay for premium outputs but receive lower-value or different-model behavior.
  • Ecosystem risk: damages trust in benchmark comparisons and model-provider reputation.

Limitations (as acknowledged in the paper)

  • Snapshot window (Sep–Dec 2025): shadow markets change rapidly over time.
  • No direct backend ground truth: inference is based on behavior/fingerprints/statistical tests.
  • Coverage is broad but not exhaustive (17 providers; selected model families).

Practical takeaway

If a project needs reliable claims about specific model behavior, use official APIs where possible. If unofficial endpoints must be used, apply explicit verification (identity tests, distribution tests, multi-run variance checks, endpoint disclosure) before treating results as valid.
